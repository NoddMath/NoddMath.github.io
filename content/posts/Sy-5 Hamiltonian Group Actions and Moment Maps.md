Throughout this chapter, $(M,\omega)$ is a symplectic manifold of dimension $2n$, and $G$ is a Lie group with Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$.

## 5.1 Symplectic group actions

We begin by recalling basic notions about group actions and their infinitesimal generators.

> [!info] Definition (smooth group action)
> A (left) **smooth action** of a Lie group $G$ on a manifold $M$ is a smooth map
> $$\Phi : G\times M \to M,\qquad \Phi(g,x) = g\cdot x,$$
> such that
> $$e\cdot x = x,\qquad (gh)\cdot x = g\cdot(h\cdot x)$$
> for all $g,h\in G$, $x\in M$, where $e$ is the identity element of $G$.

> [!info] Definition (fundamental vector fields)
> Let $G$ act smoothly on $M$. For each $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, the **fundamental vector field** $\xi_M\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$ is defined by
> $$\xi_M(x) := \left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{t=0} \exp(t\xi)\cdot x.$$
> The assignment $\xi\mapsto \xi_M$ is a Lie algebra antihomomorphism:
> $$[\xi_M,\eta_M] = -([\xi,\eta])_M.$$

When $(M,\omega)$ is symplectic, we are interested in actions that preserve $\omega$.

> [!info] Definition (symplectic action)
> A smooth action $\Phi:G\times M\to M$ is **symplectic** if
> $$\Phi_g^*\omega = \omega \quad\text{for all }g\in G,$$
> where $\Phi_g(x) := g\cdot x$.

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.1 (Infinitesimal characterization)
> Let $G$ act smoothly on $(M,\omega)$. The following are equivalent:
> 1. The action is symplectic: $\Phi_g^*\omega=\omega$ for all $g\in G$.
> 2. For every $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, the fundamental vector field $\xi_M$ is symplectic:
>    $$\mathcal{L}_{\xi_M}\omega = 0.$$
> 3. For every $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, the 1-form $\iota_{\xi_M}\omega$ is closed:
>    $$d(\iota_{\xi_M}\omega) = 0.$$

> [!success] Proof
> (1) $\Rightarrow$ (2): If $\Phi_g^*\omega=\omega$ for all $g$, then differentiating
> $\Phi_{\exp(t\xi)}^*\omega=\omega$ at $t=0$ yields $\mathcal{L}_{\xi_M}\omega=0$.  
> (2) $\Rightarrow$ (1): Conversely, if $\mathcal{L}_{\xi_M}\omega=0$ for all $\xi$, then the flow of each $\xi_M$ preserves $\omega$; since any $g$ near the identity is a product of such flows, and $G$ is generated by a neighborhood of the identity, the action preserves $\omega$.  
> (2) $\Leftrightarrow$ (3): By Cartan’s formula and $d\omega=0$,
> $$\mathcal{L}_{\xi_M}\omega = d(\iota_{\xi_M}\omega) + \iota_{\xi_M}(d\omega) = d(\iota_{\xi_M}\omega).$$
> So $\mathcal{L}_{\xi_M}\omega=0$ iff $d(\iota_{\xi_M}\omega)=0$. 

> [!example] Example 5.2 (Linear symplectic actions)
> Let $(V,\omega)$ be a symplectic vector space and $G\subset \mathrm{Sp}(V,\omega)$ a Lie subgroup of the symplectic group. Then the linear action $G\times V\to V$ is symplectic:
> $$g^*\omega = \omega \quad\text{for all }g\in G.$$
> The associated fundamental vector fields are linear vector fields on $V$, and for each $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, the 1-form $\iota_{\xi_V}\omega$ is closed (in fact exact).

## 5.2 Hamiltonian actions and moment maps

A symplectic action is “Hamiltonian” if its infinitesimal generators come from globally defined Hamiltonian functions.

> [!info] Definition (Hamiltonian $G$-action)
> Let $G$ act symplectically on $(M,\omega)$. The action is **Hamiltonian** if there exists a map
> $$\mu : M \to \mathfrak g^*$$
> such that for every $\xi\in\mathfrak g$,
> $$d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_M}\omega,$$
> where $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ is the dual pairing between $\mathfrak g^*$ and $\mathfrak g$.  
> Such a map $\mu$ is called a **moment map** (or momentum map) for the action.

Equivalently, $\xi_M$ is the Hamiltonian vector field of the function $\langle\mu,\xi\rangle$:
$$
  X_{\langle\mu,\xi\rangle} = \xi_M.
$$

> [!info] Definition (Hamiltonian $G$-space)
> A **Hamiltonian $G$-space** is a triple $(M,\omega,\mu)$ where $(M,\omega)$ is symplectic, $G$ acts symplectically on $M$, and $\mu:M\to\mathfrak g^*$ is a moment map for this action.

### 5.2.1 Equivariance and uniqueness of moment maps

Moment maps are not unique; they are determined up to constants with values in $\mathfrak g^*$ that are fixed by the coadjoint action.

Recall the **coadjoint action** $\mathrm{Ad}^*:G\to \mathrm{GL}(\mathfrak g^*)$ defined by
$$
  \langle \mathrm{Ad}^*_g\alpha,\xi\rangle
  := \langle \alpha,\mathrm{Ad}_{g^{-1}}\xi\rangle,
  \quad \alpha\in\mathfrak g^*,\ \xi\in\mathfrak g.
$$

> [!info] Definition (equivariant moment map)
> A moment map $\mu:M\to\mathfrak g^*$ is **$\mathrm{Ad}^*$-equivariant** if
> $$\mu(g\cdot x) = \mathrm{Ad}^*_g \mu(x) \quad\text{for all }g\in G,\ x\in M.$$

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.3 (Uniqueness up to central constants)
> Let $G$ act Hamiltonianly on $(M,\omega)$. If $\mu,\tilde\mu:M\to\mathfrak g^*$ are two moment maps for the same action, then
> $$\mu - \tilde\mu \equiv c$$
> is constant, and $c$ lies in the subspace
> $$(\mathfrak g^*)^G := \{ \alpha\in\mathfrak g^* \mid \mathrm{Ad}^*_g\alpha=\alpha\ \forall g\in G\}.$$

> [!success] Proof
> For each $\xi\in\mathfrak g$,
> $$d\langle\mu-\tilde\mu,\xi\rangle = d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle - d\langle\tilde\mu,\xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_M}\omega - \iota_{\xi_M}\omega = 0.$$
> Thus $\langle\mu-\tilde\mu,\xi\rangle$ is constant on each connected component of $M$. If $M$ is connected, the map
> $$\mu-\tilde\mu : M\to\mathfrak g^*$$
> is constant, say $\mu-\tilde\mu\equiv c$.  
> If $\mu$ and $\tilde\mu$ are $\mathrm{Ad}^*$-equivariant, then for all $g\in G$,
> $$c = \mu(g\cdot x)-\tilde\mu(g\cdot x) = \mathrm{Ad}^*_g\mu(x)-\mathrm{Ad}^*_g\tilde\mu(x) = \mathrm{Ad}^*_g(\mu(x)-\tilde\mu(x)) = \mathrm{Ad}^*_g c.$$
> Hence $c\in(\mathfrak g^*)^G$. 

Existence of an $\mathrm{Ad}^*$-equivariant moment map is a subtle global question; for compact $G$ it often holds after a suitable shift by a central constant.

## 5.3 Examples: circle and torus actions

We now compute several standard moment maps explicitly.

### 5.3.1 The circle acting on the 2-sphere

Let $(S^2,\omega)$ be the standard 2-sphere with area form, normalized so that $\int_{S^2}\omega = 4\pi$. Identify $S^2\subset\mathbb{R}^3$ with coordinates $(x,y,z)$. The circle $S^1$ acts by rotation around the $z$-axis:
$$
  e^{\mathrm{i}\theta}\cdot (x,y,z)
  = (\cos\theta\,x - \sin\theta\,y,\ \sin\theta\,x + \cos\theta\,y,\ z).
$$

The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{u}(1)\simeq\mathbb{R}$ is generated by $\xi=1$, whose fundamental vector field $\xi_{S^2}$ is the velocity field of rotation. In spherical coordinates $(\varphi,\theta)$ with $z=\cos\varphi$, one has
$$
  \omega = \sin\varphi\, d\varphi\wedge d\theta.
$$

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.4 (Moment map for rotation of $S^2$)
> The function
> $$\mu:S^2\to\mathbb{R},\qquad \mu(x,y,z)=z,$$
> is a moment map for the $S^1$-action (up to an additive constant multiple).

> [!success] Proof
> In spherical coordinates, the fundamental vector field of rotation is $\xi_{S^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}$. Then
> $$\iota_{\xi_{S^2}}\omega = \iota_{\partial/\partial\theta}(\sin\varphi\, d\varphi\wedge d\theta) = -\sin\varphi\, d\varphi.$$
> On the other hand, the height function $z=\cos\varphi$ satisfies
> $$dz = -\sin\varphi\, d\varphi.$$
> Hence $\iota_{\xi_{S^2}}\omega = dz = d\mu$. Thus $\mu$ is a moment map. Adding a constant $c$ gives another moment map $\mu+c$. 

Geometrically, the image $\mu(S^2) = [-1,1]$ is the interval between the south and north poles, and the level sets $\mu^{-1}(a)$ are circles of latitude (except for the poles).

### 5.3.2 Torus actions on $\mathbb{C}^n$

Let $\mathbb{C}^n\simeq\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ with coordinates $z_j=x_j+\mathrm{i}y_j$, and symplectic form
$$
  \omega_0 = \sum_{j=1}^n dx_j\wedge dy_j
           = \frac{\mathrm{i}}{2}\sum_{j=1}^n dz_j\wedge d\overline{z_j}.
$$

Consider the standard $T^n$-action
$$
  (e^{\mathrm{i}\theta_1},\dots,e^{\mathrm{i}\theta_n})\cdot (z_1,\dots,z_n)
  = (e^{\mathrm{i}\theta_1}z_1,\dots,e^{\mathrm{i}\theta_n}z_n).
$$

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.5 (Moment map for the standard $T^n$-action)
> The map $\mu:\mathbb{C}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$ defined by
> $$\mu(z_1,\dots,z_n) = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(|z_1|^2,\dots,|z_n|^2\bigr)$$
> is a moment map for this action (up to an additive constant vector).

> [!success] Proof
> Let $\xi=(\xi_1,\dots,\xi_n)\in\mathbb{R}^n$ be in the Lie algebra of $T^n$. The corresponding fundamental vector field is
> $$\xi_{\mathbb{C}^n} = \sum_{j=1}^n \xi_j\,\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j},$$
> where $z_j = r_je^{\mathrm{i}\theta_j}$. In polar coordinates $(r_j,\theta_j)$,
> $$\omega_0 = \sum_{j=1}^n r_j\,dr_j\wedge d\theta_j.$$
> Then
> $$\iota_{\xi_{\mathbb{C}^n}}\omega_0 = \sum_{j=1}^n \xi_j\,r_j\, dr_j = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^n \xi_j\, d(r_j^2) = d\left(\frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^n \xi_j r_j^2\right).$$
> On the other hand,
> $$\langle\mu(z),\xi\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^n \xi_j |z_j|^2 = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^n \xi_j r_j^2.$$
> Thus $d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle = \iota_{\xi_{\mathbb{C}^n}}\omega_0$ for all $\xi$, so $\mu$ is a moment map. 

### 5.3.3 Torus actions on complex projective space

Let $\mathbb{C}P^n$ with homogeneous coordinates $[z_0:\dots:z_n]$, endowed with the Fubini–Study form $\omega_{\mathrm{FS}}$. The torus $T^{n+1}$ acts on $\mathbb{C}^{n+1}$ by diagonal multiplication, and this descends to a Hamiltonian $T^n$-action on $\mathbb{C}P^n$ after factoring out the diagonal $S^1$. The resulting effective $T^n$-action rotates the homogeneous coordinates.

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.6 (Moment map for $\mathbb{C}P^n$)
> With a suitable normalization of $\omega_{\mathrm{FS}}$, a moment map
> $\mu:\mathbb{C}P^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$ for the effective $T^n$-action is given by
> $$\mu([z_0:\dots:z_n]) = \frac{1}{\sum_{j=0}^n |z_j|^2} \bigl(|z_1|^2,\dots,|z_n|^2\bigr)$$
> (up to an overall affine transformation), and its image is the standard simplex
> $$\Delta^n = \left\{ (x_1,\dots,x_n)\in\mathbb{R}^n \,\middle|\, x_j\ge 0,\ \sum_{j=1}^n x_j \le 1 \right\}.$$

We defer a detailed derivation (involving pulling back from $\mathbb{C}^{n+1}$ and symplectic reduction) to the chapter on toric manifolds, but it is a good exercise to check the moment map condition in affine coordinates.

## 5.4 Cotangent-lifted actions and coadjoint orbits

Two major sources of Hamiltonian $G$-spaces are cotangent bundles of configuration spaces and coadjoint orbits.

### 5.4.1 The canonical moment map on $T^*Q$

Let $G$ act smoothly on a manifold $Q$. There is a natural lifted action on the cotangent bundle
$$
  G\times T^*Q\to T^*Q,\quad
  g\cdot (q,\alpha_q) := (g\cdot q,\ (d\Phi_{g^{-1}})^*_q \alpha_q),
$$
where $\Phi_g:Q\to Q$ is the action on $Q$. This lifted action preserves the canonical symplectic form $\omega_{\mathrm{can}}$ on $T^*Q$.

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.7 (Canonical moment map on $T^*Q$)
> Let $G$ act on $Q$, and let $(T^*Q,\omega_{\mathrm{can}})$ be its cotangent bundle with canonical symplectic form. For $(q,\alpha_q)\in T^*Q$ and $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, define
> $$\langle\mu(q,\alpha_q),\xi\rangle := \alpha_q\bigl(\xi_Q(q)\bigr),$$
> where $\xi_Q$ is the fundamental vector field on $Q$ induced by $\xi$. Then:
> 1. $\mu:T^*Q\to\mathfrak g^*$ is smooth and $G$-equivariant;
> 2. $\mu$ is a moment map for the lifted $G$-action on $(T^*Q,\omega_{\mathrm{can}})$.

> [!success] Proof (idea)
> Smoothness is clear from the definition; equivariance follows from the naturality of the lift and the definition of the coadjoint action. To verify the moment map identity, we use the tautological 1-form $\lambda\in\Omega^1(T^*Q)$ and the fact that $\omega_{\mathrm{can}}=-d\lambda$. For $\xi\in\mathfrak g$ and $v\in T_{(q,\alpha_q)}T^*Q$,
> $$\iota_{\xi_{T^*Q}}\omega_{\mathrm{can}}(v) = -\,\mathcal{L}_{\xi_{T^*Q}}\lambda(v) + d(\iota_{\xi_{T^*Q}}\lambda)(v).$$
> One checks that $\mathcal{L}_{\xi_{T^*Q}}\lambda=0$ and $\iota_{\xi_{T^*Q}}\lambda = \langle\mu,\xi\rangle$, so
> $$\iota_{\xi_{T^*Q}}\omega_{\mathrm{can}} = d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle,$$
> as required. Detailed calculations in local coordinates can be found in standard references.

This construction is ubiquitous in classical mechanics: $Q$ is the configuration space, $T^*Q$ the phase space, and symmetries of $Q$ give Hamiltonian symmetries of $T^*Q$ with momentum map as above.

### 5.4.2 Coadjoint orbits and the KKS form

Let $G$ be a Lie group with Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$. The coadjoint action of $G$ on $\mathfrak g^*$ gives **coadjoint orbits**:

> [!info] Definition (coadjoint orbit)
> For $\eta\in\mathfrak g^*$, the **coadjoint orbit** of $\eta$ is
> $$\mathcal{O}_\eta := \{ \mathrm{Ad}^*_g\eta \mid g\in G\} \subset \mathfrak g^*.$$
> It is an immersed submanifold of $\mathfrak g^*$, diffeomorphic to $G/G_\eta$, where $G_\eta$ is the stabilizer of $\eta$.

The coadjoint orbit carries a canonical $G$-invariant 2-form, the Kirillov–Kostant–Souriau (KKS) form. For $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, the fundamental vector field on $\mathcal{O}_\eta$ is
$$
  \xi_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha)
  = \left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{0} \mathrm{Ad}^*_{\exp(t\xi)}\alpha.
$$

> [!info] Definition (KKS 2-form)
> For $\alpha\in\mathcal{O}_\eta$ and $\xi,\zeta\in\mathfrak g$, define
> $$\omega_{\mathrm{KKS},\alpha}\bigl(\xi_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha), \zeta_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha)\bigr) := \langle \alpha, [\xi,\zeta]\rangle.$$
> This defines a 2-form $\omega_{\mathrm{KKS}}$ on $\mathcal{O}_\eta$.

> [!note] Theorem 5.8 (Coadjoint orbits are Hamiltonian $G$-spaces)
> For each coadjoint orbit $\mathcal{O}_\eta\subset\mathfrak g^*$:
> 1. $\omega_{\mathrm{KKS}}$ is a well-defined smooth 2-form on $\mathcal{O}_\eta$;
> 2. $\omega_{\mathrm{KKS}}$ is closed and nondegenerate, so $(\mathcal{O}_\eta,\omega_{\mathrm{KKS}})$ is a symplectic manifold;
> 3. The coadjoint action of $G$ on $\mathcal{O}_\eta$ is Hamiltonian with moment map the inclusion
>    $$\mu:\mathcal{O}_\eta\hookrightarrow\mathfrak g^*.$$

> [!success] Proof (idea)
> Well-definedness and smoothness follow from $G$-invariance and the fact that tangent vectors to the orbit are of the form $\xi_{\mathcal{O}}$. Nondegeneracy of $\omega_{\mathrm{KKS}}$ is equivalent to the condition that the isotropy Lie algebra $\mathfrak g_\alpha$ is exactly the symplectic orthogonal of $\mathfrak g \cdot \alpha$. Closedness can be checked using the Maurer–Cartan equation and $G$-invariance.  
> For the moment map property, note that for $\xi\in\mathfrak g$,
> $$\langle\mu(\alpha),\xi\rangle = \langle\alpha,\xi\rangle,$$
> and differentiation along $\zeta_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha)$ yields
> $$d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle(\zeta_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha)) = \langle\alpha,[\zeta,\xi]\rangle = \omega_{\mathrm{KKS},\alpha}(\xi_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha),\zeta_{\mathcal{O}}(\alpha)),$$
> which is the moment map identity.

Coadjoint orbits play a central role in representation theory and geometric quantization; here they serve as a rich class of explicit Hamiltonian $G$-spaces.

## 5.5 Obstructions to Hamiltonian actions

Not every symplectic $G$-action is Hamiltonian. We now describe the basic cohomological obstruction.

Let $G$ act symplectically on $(M,\omega)$. For each $\xi\in\mathfrak g$,
$$
  \alpha_\xi := \iota_{\xi_M}\omega \in \Omega^1(M)
$$
is closed (by Proposition 5.1). The assignment $\xi\mapsto [\alpha_\xi]\in H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ is linear.

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.9 (Cohomological obstruction)
> Let $G$ act symplectically on $(M,\omega)$. Define
> $$\psi : \mathfrak g \to H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(M),\qquad \psi(\xi) := [\iota_{\xi_M}\omega].$$
> Then:
> 1. $\psi$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism (for the trivial Lie algebra structure on $H^1$);
> 2. the action is Hamiltonian if and only if $\psi=0$, i.e. $[\iota_{\xi_M}\omega]=0$ for all $\xi\in\mathfrak g$.

> [!success] Proof
> For (1), linearity is clear; since $H^1$ is abelian, any linear map is a Lie algebra homomorphism.  
> For (2), if the action is Hamiltonian with moment map $\mu$, then
> $$\iota_{\xi_M}\omega = d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle,$$
> so $[\iota_{\xi_M}\omega]=0$ for all $\xi$. Conversely, if $[\iota_{\xi_M}\omega]=0$ for all $\xi$, we can choose functions $H_\xi$ with
> $$\iota_{\xi_M}\omega = dH_\xi.$$
> By adjusting by constants and using linearity in $\xi$, we can arrange that $\xi\mapsto H_\xi$ is linear; then $\mu(x)\in\mathfrak g^*$, defined by $\langle\mu(x),\xi\rangle:=H_\xi(x)$, is a moment map.

Two simple corollaries are worth recording.

> [!abstract] Corollary 5.10 (Vanishing $H^1$ implies all actions Hamiltonian)
> If $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)=0$, then every symplectic $G$-action on $(M,\omega)$ is Hamiltonian.

> [!success] Proof
> If $H^1(M)=0$, every closed 1-form is exact, so $[\iota_{\xi_M}\omega]=0$ for all $\xi$. By Proposition 5.9, the action is Hamiltonian. 

> [!abstract] Corollary 5.11 (Semisimple group actions)
> Suppose $G$ is compact and semisimple, and acts symplectically on $(M,\omega)$. Then under mild hypotheses (e.g. $H^1(M)^G = 0$), every symplectic $G$-action is Hamiltonian.

In practice, one often uses averaging over a compact group to arrange $G$-invariance and then applies the vanishing of appropriate cohomology groups to produce a moment map.

> [!example] Example 5.12 (A symplectic but non-Hamiltonian torus action)
> Let $M=T^2$ with coordinates $(\theta_1,\theta_2)$ and symplectic form $\omega=d\theta_1\wedge d\theta_2$. The torus $T^2$ acts on itself by translations:
> $$(\phi_1,\phi_2)\cdot(\theta_1,\theta_2) = (\theta_1+\phi_1,\theta_2+\phi_2).$$
> This action is symplectic, since translations preserve $\omega$. For the infinitesimal generators
> $$X_1 = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_1},\quad X_2 = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_2},$$
> we have
> $$\iota_{X_1}\omega = d\theta_2,\quad \iota_{X_2}\omega = -d\theta_1.$$
> These 1-forms represent nonzero classes in $H^1(T^2;\mathbb{R})$, hence are not exact. Thus $\psi\neq 0$ and the action is not Hamiltonian.

This example shows that $H^1(M)\neq 0$ is a genuine obstruction to the existence of a global moment map.

## 5.6 Moment maps and Lie algebra structures

Moment maps interact beautifully with the Lie algebra and Poisson structures introduced in Chapter 4.

Let $(M,\omega,\mu)$ be a Hamiltonian $G$-space. For $\xi\in\mathfrak g$, denote
$$
  H_\xi := \langle\mu,\xi\rangle \in C^\infty(M).
$$

### 5.6.1 Lie algebra representation via Hamiltonian vector fields

> [!abstract] Proposition 5.13 (Compatibility with Poisson bracket)
> Let $(M,\omega,\mu)$ be a Hamiltonian $G$-space. Then for all $\xi,\zeta\in\mathfrak g$,
> $$\{H_\xi,H_\zeta\} = H_{[\xi,\zeta]} + \mathrm{const.}$$
> If $\mu$ is $\mathrm{Ad}^*$-equivariant, then the constant term vanishes and
> $$\{H_\xi,H_\zeta\} = H_{[\xi,\zeta]}.$$

> [!success] Proof
> We have $X_{H_\xi} = \xi_M$ and $X_{H_\zeta} = \zeta_M$. From Proposition 4.11,
> $$[X_{H_\xi},X_{H_\zeta}] = -X_{\{H_\xi,H_\zeta\}}.$$
> On the other hand, the fundamental vector fields satisfy
> $$[\xi_M,\zeta_M] = -([\xi,\zeta])_M.$$
> Thus
> $$-X_{\{H_\xi,H_\zeta\}} = [X_{H_\xi},X_{H_\zeta}] = [\xi_M,\zeta_M] = -([\xi,\zeta])_M = -X_{H_{[\xi,\zeta]}}.$$
> Hence $X_{\{H_\xi,H_\zeta\}} = X_{H_{[\xi,\zeta]}}$, so
> $$d\bigl(\{H_\xi,H_\zeta\} - H_{[\xi,\zeta]}\bigr) = 0.$$
> On a connected component of $M$, the difference is constant. If $\mu$ is $\mathrm{Ad}^*$-equivariant, one can check that this constant must be zero by evaluating at a fixed point of the action (if any) or by using averaging arguments.

Thus, for an equivariant moment map, $\xi\mapsto H_\xi$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism
$$
  \mathfrak g \to \bigl(C^\infty(M),\{\cdot,\cdot\}\bigr).
$$

### 5.6.2 The dual picture: Poisson maps

The dual viewpoint is that moment maps are Poisson maps from $(M,\omega)$ to the dual Lie algebra with its linear Poisson structure.

Recall that $\mathfrak g^*$ carries the **Lie–Poisson bracket**: for linear functions $\ell_\xi(\alpha)=\langle\alpha,\xi\rangle$,
$$
  \{\ell_\xi,\ell_\zeta\}_{\mathfrak g^*}
  = \ell_{[\xi,\zeta]}.
$$
This extends uniquely to a Poisson bracket on $C^\infty(\mathfrak g^*)$.

> [!info] Definition (Poisson map)
> Let $(P,\{\cdot,\cdot\}_P)$, $(Q,\{\cdot,\cdot\}_Q)$ be Poisson manifolds. A smooth map $f:P\to Q$ is a **Poisson map** if
> $$\{F\circ f,G\circ f\}_P = \{F,G\}_Q\circ f$$
> for all $F,G\in C^\infty(Q)$.

> [!note] Theorem 5.14 (Moment map as Poisson map)
> Let $(M,\omega,\mu)$ be a Hamiltonian $G$-space with $\mathrm{Ad}^*$-equivariant moment map $\mu:M\to\mathfrak g^*$. Equip $\mathfrak g^*$ with its Lie–Poisson structure. Then:
> 1. $\mu$ is a Poisson map:
>    $$\{F\circ\mu,G\circ\mu\}_M = \{F,G\}_{\mathfrak g^*}\circ\mu \quad \forall F,G\in C^\infty(\mathfrak g^*);$$
> 2. Coadjoint orbits $\mathcal{O}\subset\mathfrak g^*$ are the symplectic leaves of $\mathfrak g^*$; the KKS form is the induced symplectic form on leaves.

> [!success] Proof (sketch)
> It suffices to check the Poisson property for linear functions $\ell_\xi,\ell_\zeta$, since these generate the Lie–Poisson structure. On the one hand,
> $$\{\ell_\xi\circ\mu,\ell_\zeta\circ\mu\}_M = \{H_\xi,H_\zeta\}_M = H_{[\xi,\zeta]} = \ell_{[\xi,\zeta]}\circ\mu,$$
> using Proposition 5.13. On the other hand,
> $$\{\ell_\xi,\ell_\zeta\}_{\mathfrak g^*} = \ell_{[\xi,\zeta]}.$$
> Thus the identity holds for linear functions, and by standard arguments extends to all smooth functions. The description of symplectic leaves as coadjoint orbits is classical and follows from the definition of the Lie–Poisson bracket.

- constructed the canonical moment map on cotangent bundles and described coadjoint orbits with the KKS form as fundamental Hamiltonian $G$-spaces;

- explained how moment maps intertwine the Lie algebra and Poisson structures, and how they are Poisson maps into $\mathfrak g^*$.
